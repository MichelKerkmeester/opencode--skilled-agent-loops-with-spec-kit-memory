# Iteration 003: Real Review Artifact Rich-Field Survey

## Focus

Inspect real completed deep-review packets and delta/state files to measure whether rich fields are present in practice: `graphEvents`, `findingDetails`, `findingClass`, `scopeProof`, `affectedSurfaceHints`, counterevidence, producer/consumer evidence, and bug-class-style coverage.

## Actions Taken

1. Read the current strategy plus iterations 001 and 002 to preserve the working hypothesis: deep-review has stronger post-discovery validation than pre-discovery bug candidate generation.
2. Enumerated review artifacts under `.opencode/specs/**/review/**`, then searched those JSONL and markdown files plus `.opencode/skills/deep-review/scripts/tests/fixtures/**` for `graphEvents`, `findingDetails`, `findingClass`, `scopeProof`, `affectedSurfaceHints`, counterevidence, producer/consumer, bug-class, and invariant language.
3. Parsed 476 review/fixture JSONL files and 885 completed or legacy iteration records to measure field presence. This was a local structural count across review state and delta logs, not a semantic judgment of whether every finding was valid.
4. Read representative high-signal artifacts with line numbers: current completed review packets, older sparse delta files, graph-rich review deltas, and the reducer blocked-stop fixture.

## Findings

1. Rich fields exist in real packets, but they are not universal enough to be treated as normal deep-review output. The local JSONL survey found 885 completed or legacy iteration records. Of those, 220 had a `findingDetails` field and 186 had non-empty details. Across 537 individual `findingDetails` entries, 280 had `findingClass`, 142 had `scopeProof`, and 138 had non-empty `affectedSurfaceHints`. A strong positive example is the council graph support review: iteration 1 includes two findings with `findingClass`, `scopeProof`, and `affectedSurfaceHints`, plus five `graphEvents` [SOURCE: .opencode/specs/skilled-agent-orchestration/101-deep-multi-ai-council-skill/003-deep-ai-council-graph-support/review/deep-review-state.jsonl:2].

2. Sparse completed review records are also common in current packets. The small-model optimization review has early complete iterations with `findingsNew: []`, no `findingDetails`, and empty `graphEvents` [SOURCE: .opencode/specs/skilled-agent-orchestration/z_archive/093-small-ai-model-optimization/review/deep-review-state.jsonl:3]. Its high-volume security pass records 10 findings as inline `findingsNew` objects, but still has no `findingDetails`, no `findingClass`, no `scopeProof`, no affected-surface hints, and empty `graphEvents` [SOURCE: .opencode/specs/skilled-agent-orchestration/z_archive/093-small-ai-model-optimization/review/deep-review-state.jsonl:5]. This is the concrete version of the iteration-002 schema gap: important findings can be logged in a shallower shape.

3. `findingsNew` is inconsistent in real artifacts, not just in documentation. The survey found completed records using arrays, numbers, and severity-count objects for `findingsNew`: 181 arrays, 35 numbers, and 446 objects. A sparse delta records `findingsNew: 5` beside five `findingDetails` entries that only carry severity/category/title/file/evidence [SOURCE: .opencode/specs/skilled-agent-orchestration/z_archive/082-sk-doc-skill-readme-and-structure/review/deltas/iter-001.jsonl:1]. The small-model review uses both `findingsNew: []` and arrays of finding objects in the same state log [SOURCE: .opencode/specs/skilled-agent-orchestration/z_archive/093-small-ai-model-optimization/review/deep-review-state.jsonl:3] [SOURCE: .opencode/specs/skilled-agent-orchestration/z_archive/093-small-ai-model-optimization/review/deep-review-state.jsonl:5]. This makes reducer consumers tolerate shape drift instead of forcing deeper evidence.

4. `graphEvents` are present often enough to prove the mechanism works, but absent or empty often enough that graph convergence cannot be assumed. The survey found 208 completed records with a `graphEvents` field and only 40 with non-empty events. The causal graph routing review can produce a rich graph inventory with file, hotspot, and dimension nodes [SOURCE: .opencode/specs/system-spec-kit/026-graph-and-context-optimization/009-causal-graph-channel-routing/001-deliver-causal-graph-channel-routing-mvp/review/deltas/iter-001.jsonl:1]. By contrast, the council graph security iteration has a rich P1 finding and claim adjudication but `graphEvents: []` [SOURCE: .opencode/specs/skilled-agent-orchestration/101-deep-multi-ai-council-skill/003-deep-ai-council-graph-support/review/deep-review-state.jsonl:5], and the small-model review keeps empty graph events across many iterations [SOURCE: .opencode/specs/skilled-agent-orchestration/z_archive/093-small-ai-model-optimization/review/deep-review-state.jsonl:3].

5. Counterevidence appears mostly inside claim-adjudication style packets, not as a general finding requirement. The survey found only 20 detail entries with counterevidence-style fields, all via direct `counterevidenceSought` or `claimAdjudication.counterevidenceSought`. The council graph security finding is a good example: it records evidence refs, counterevidence sought, an alternative explanation, final severity, confidence, and downgrade trigger [SOURCE: .opencode/specs/skilled-agent-orchestration/101-deep-multi-ai-council-skill/003-deep-ai-council-graph-support/review/deep-review-state.jsonl:5]. The shallower parent review delta has multiple findings but no comparable counterevidence or adjudication fields [SOURCE: .opencode/specs/skilled-agent-orchestration/z_archive/082-sk-doc-skill-readme-and-structure/review/deltas/iter-001.jsonl:1].

6. Producer/consumer evidence exists as free text and classifications, but not as a mandatory matrix. The survey found 115 detail entries whose serialized content mentions producer or consumer, often through classes like `cross-consumer` or affected-surface hints. The council graph examples show useful cross-consumer `scopeProof` [SOURCE: .opencode/specs/skilled-agent-orchestration/101-deep-multi-ai-council-skill/003-deep-ai-council-graph-support/review/deep-review-state.jsonl:2]. But I found no structured producer node, consumer node, producer/consumer matrix, or required flow-coverage field in actual reviewed JSONL outputs. That keeps producer/consumer tracing optional and prose-dependent.

7. Explicit bug-class-style coverage is effectively absent from the surveyed structured details. The JSONL parse found zero `findingDetails` entries matching `bug-class`, `bug class`, or `bugClass`, and only two mentioning invariant. This matters because the current research question asks whether deep-review requires hypothesis rotation and bug-class coverage [SOURCE: .opencode/specs/skilled-agent-orchestration/116-deep-review-complexity/research/deep-research-strategy.md:23]. In practice, actual review artifacts mostly encode review dimension, severity, file evidence, and sometimes cross-consumer scope, not an explicit "classes of bugs searched and ruled out" ledger.

8. The deep-review fixture coverage supports iteration 002's conclusion: tests prove blocked-stop bookkeeping more than deep bug discovery. The blocked-stop fixture's complete iterations include dimensions, files, findings counts, severity summaries, and convergence signals, but no `findingDetails`, no `graphEvents`, and no rich field coverage [SOURCE: .opencode/skills/deep-review/scripts/tests/fixtures/blocked-stop-session/review/deep-review-state.jsonl:2] [SOURCE: .opencode/skills/deep-review/scripts/tests/fixtures/blocked-stop-session/review/deep-review-state.jsonl:4]. Its event proves stop blocking on missing dimensions and active P0 [SOURCE: .opencode/skills/deep-review/scripts/tests/fixtures/blocked-stop-session/review/deep-review-state.jsonl:5], not candidate-generation depth.

## Questions Answered

- How often do real deep-review packets omit `graphEvents`, malformed or mixed-shape `findingsNew`, or shallow `findingDetails` while still completing? Often. In the surveyed completed/legacy iteration records, non-empty `graphEvents` appeared in only 40 of 885, `findingDetails` appeared in 220 of 885, and `findingsNew` had at least three real shapes.
- Are rich fields present in practice? Yes, but unevenly. Some packets have high-quality `findingClass`, `scopeProof`, `affectedSurfaceHints`, and claim adjudication. Other complete packets record important findings in shallower `findingsNew` arrays or number counts.
- Does actual output include bug-class-style coverage? I found no explicit bug-class field or phrase in structured `findingDetails`, and only two invariant mentions. That is the strongest empirical support so far for adding a bug-class/hypothesis matrix rather than relying on dimension rotation.
- Why can focused deep-research surface more bugs? Because focused research can keep a narrow bug hypothesis active across iterations, while deep-review artifacts show that a review iteration can complete with dimension/file/severity bookkeeping and no structured proof that candidate bug classes, producer/consumer paths, or invariants were exhaustively searched.

## Questions Remaining

- Should the first implementation change be stricter schema validation for `findingsNew` and `findingDetails`, or a new required per-dimension bug-class/search-matrix artifact?
- Should `graphEvents` become mandatory above a target complexity threshold, or should deep-review allow graphless runs but require a substitute matrix with file, producer, consumer, invariant, and negative-test rows?
- What exact JSON schema should represent bug-class coverage without making low-risk documentation reviews too heavy?
- How should existing review packets be migrated or tolerated if new strict fields become required?
