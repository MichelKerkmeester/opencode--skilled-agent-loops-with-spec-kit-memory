# Cognitive Laws for Interface Design

Eight shared cognitive laws that design children cite as concise rationale for hierarchy, interaction, motion, and audit decisions.

---

## 1. OVERVIEW

### Purpose

Give design children a compact, citable rationale set for making and auditing design choices. These laws are shared vocabulary, not a complete UX research method.

### Usage

Reference a law by name when justifying a hierarchy, interaction, timing, or audit decision. Children own the workflow that applies each law.

---

## 2. Hick's Law

Decision time rises as choices increase. Reduce visible options, group related actions, and expose advanced choices progressively when a surface feels slow or indecisive.

## 3. Miller's Law

Working memory is limited. Chunk information into meaningful groups instead of asking users to compare many isolated items at once.

## 4. Fitts's Law

Targets are faster to hit when they are larger and closer. Make primary controls easy to acquire, especially on touch screens and in repeated workflows.

## 5. Doherty Threshold

Interfaces feel responsive when feedback arrives quickly enough to preserve flow. Use immediate state feedback, optimistic affordances, or progress cues when work cannot complete instantly.

## 6. Aesthetic-Usability Effect

People often perceive attractive interfaces as easier to use. Visual polish can improve tolerance, but it must not hide broken hierarchy, unclear affordances, or accessibility failures.

## 7. Von Restorff Effect

Distinctive items are more memorable. Use contrast to make the one important action or message stand out, not to make every element louder.

## 8. Law of Proximity

Items near each other are perceived as related. Use spacing to communicate grouping before adding lines, boxes, or labels.

## 9. Law of Common Region

Items inside the same bounded area are perceived as related. Use cards, panels, fieldsets, and background regions to clarify relationships when proximity alone is not enough.

---

## 10. Application Notes

- `interface` cites these laws when justifying hierarchy and direction.
- `foundations` cites them when shaping density, grouping, and token systems.
- `motion` cites them when choosing feedback timing and reduced-motion alternatives.
- `audit` cites them when explaining design-quality findings.
